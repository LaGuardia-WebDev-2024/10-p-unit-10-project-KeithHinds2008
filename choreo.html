Choreography, in its most intricate and dynamic form, is the deliberate orchestration of movement through time and space, an embodied language where gestures become syntax and rhythm becomes narrative. It is not merely the arrangement of steps, but rather the architectural blueprint of human expression — a kinetic script that intertwines emotion, culture, history, and imagination. At its core, choreography is both an art and an inquiry: it asks how the body can speak what the voice cannot, how movement can archive memory, and how collective motion can summon identity and transformation.

Choreographers are both composers and storytellers, crafting spatial dialogues that oscillate between abstraction and specificity. They mold movement into metaphors, drawing from diverse disciplines — music, theater, fashion, politics — to construct performances that pulse with social commentary, emotional resonance, and visual spectacle. Whether grounded in the personal or the universal, choreography is a living archive, shaped by the body’s resistance, resilience, and resonance.


My personal understanding and appreciation of choreography is deeply shaped by two formidable figures in the dance world: Camille A. Brown and Parris Goebel.

Camille A. Brown is a visionary choreographer whose work redefines the relationship between dance, history, and narrative. Her piece City of Rain stands as a profound example of dance as a vessel for tribute and remembrance. Dedicated to a friend who passed away, the piece becomes a sacred elegy — a spiritual and emotional offering articulated through rich ensemble work, sweeping gestures, and nuanced interplay between grief and grace. Brown’s choreography often explores African-American identity, ancestral memory, and vernacular movement with breathtaking clarity, making the personal political and the political poetic.

In contrast, Parris Goebel is a powerhouse of contemporary commercial choreography. Her style is visceral, explosive, and unapologetically bold, often fusing street styles with theatrical flair and narrative punch. Goebel’s influence spans global pop culture, having worked with some of the biggest names in the industry, including Jennifer Lopez, Justin Bieber, Janet Jackson, Ciara, and perhaps most notably, Rihanna. She has become a central creative force behind Rihanna’s Savage X Fenty fashion shows — groundbreaking productions that blend choreography, fashion, and diverse body representation into immersive, multi-sensory spectacles. Goebel’s work in this space has redefined what commercial choreography can look like, transforming runway into ritual, performance into empowerment.

Together, Brown and Goebel represent a broad yet intersecting spectrum of choreographic artistry — one rooted in cultural introspection and narrative, the other in commercial innovation and fierce individuality. Both use movement to challenge norms, celebrate identity, and articulate stories that live in the body, reminding us that choreography is not just seen — it is felt, remembered, and lived.
